What is artificial life?

Artificial life is a bottom up form of modelling biological systems. Therough the interactions of simplistic automatons and the tuning of their invidivual beahviours, interesting global dynamics emerge that resemble those seen in life. The goal of these systems is to generate these emergant, lifelike, behaviours using the simplest possible method. By constructing these models, a greater understanding of how simple base components can be responsible for highly complex global emergant behaviours.

There are three main categories in artificial life: (1) soft[ware], (2) hard[ware], and (3) wet[ware]. Soft artificial life systems are implemented in software and simply emulate behaviours in a simulated environment (e.g. the system described in [1], which is discussed later). Hard artificial life are generally robots that are controlled by an artificial life algorithm. Finally, wet artificial life systems are systems that use a bioligical controller as part of the algorithm, for example the robots discussed in [2, 3].

The fish simulation showcased here builds upon an artificial life simulation known as boids [1]. Boids work by following three simple principles:

  1. Separation: Each agent should avoid crowding its neighbours.
  2. Cohesion: Each agent should attempt to head toward the position of its neighbours.
  3. Alignment: Each agent should head in the same direction as its neighbours.
